What cold outreach actually taught me

June 21, 2026 · Liam Rodgers

I spent part of June cold emailing finance professionals around Charleston. People I had never met, asking if they would be willing to talk to a high school student about how they got into the industry and what they actually do day to day. I wanted real exposure to the field, and I figured the fastest way to get it was to ask for it directly instead of waiting for it to show up.

Most of them never replied. That part stung at first. You research the person, write something, proofread it, hit send, and then nothing comes back. The honest lesson is that the silence is not personal. These are busy people, a cold email from a teenager is easy to skip, and no response is the default, not a verdict on you.

The replies I did get had a pattern. The emails that landed were the ones where I had clearly done my homework on the person, mentioned something specific about their firm or their path instead of sending an obvious template, and asked for something small and easy to say yes to, like fifteen minutes of their time rather than ongoing mentorship. Specific and low effort to answer beat polished but generic every single time.

I also learned not to treat the first email as the whole attempt. A short, polite follow-up about a week later pulled responses that the first email never did. Not nagging, just a reminder, because the first message usually just got buried under everything else in their inbox.

One of those conversations turned into a coffee chat with a managing partner at a sector-focused private equity firm. One yes out of a lot of sends. That ratio sounds rough until you remember that I only needed one for the whole thing to be worth it.

My biggest takeaway is that cold outreach is a numbers game with a craft layer sitting on top of it. The volume matters, but so does treating each person like a person instead of a name on a list. The rejection, or more often the silence, is just the cost of doing it at all. I would rather send a stack of emails and get a few real conversations out of it than sit around waiting for opportunities to find me, because they will not.
